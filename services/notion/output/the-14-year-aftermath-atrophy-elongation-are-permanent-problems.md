## Content overview


The 14-Year Aftermath: Atrophy & Elongation are Permanent Problems 🦵



While patients often report excellent subjective recovery (low ATRS scores) after Achilles Tendon Rupture (**ATR**), objective data reveals that significant structural and functional deficits can persist for over a decade.


**The Key Details: Long-Term Deficits 📉**

📅 **Published:** 2017 (The Journal of Bone and Joint Surgery - _Western Authors: Heikkinen et al._)
 **Muscle Atrophy:** Even 14 years post-surgery, patients exhibited persistent deficits of **11% to 13% in soleus and gastrocnemius muscle volume** in the injured leg.3

**Tendon Elongation:** The injured Achilles tendon was, on average, **12 mm longer** than the uninjured side.5

T**he Link:** The study found a strong correlation: **Increased Achilles tendon elongation is associated with both calf muscle atrophy and persistent strength deficits** (**12% to 18%** weaker).


### **Clinical Insight** 🎯


This research confirms that recovery is often incomplete on a functional level, even if the patient feels subjectively recovered. The key takeaway for rehabilitation specialists is that **restoring calf muscle volume**—particularly in the soleus—and preventing tendon lengthening are the primary determinants of long-term strength and function, requiring focused, high-intensity training programs for years, not just months.


**Question:** What methods are your physical therapists using to quantify and address the persistent Soleus deficit in post-ATR patients beyond the 2-year mark?
