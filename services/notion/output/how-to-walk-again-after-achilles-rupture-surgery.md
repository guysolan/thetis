# **How to walk again after Achilles rupture surgery**


**Recovering from Achilles rupture surgery takes time and patience.** Here’s what you need to know to get back on your feet:

1. **Timeline**: Full recovery takes 6–12 months. Walking starts in phases:
    1. **Weeks 1–2**: No weight on the leg; focus on healing.
    2. **Weeks 3–6**: Gradual weight-bearing with a protective boot.
    3. **Weeks 7–10**: Learn to walk in the boot and transition to regular shoes.
    4. **Weeks 10+**: Walk independently and strengthen your Achilles.
2. **Protective Gear**:
    1. Use a **walking boot** (e.g., [Vacoped](https://opedmedical.com/products/vacoped/?srsltid=AfmBOorgcm6p9uPI5exoJ15lC03mc6O36H59-CpQVk1XxiqWk27BVx7B): $350, [Aircast](https://aircastboot.com/): $225) to support your tendon.
    2. Heel lifts and night splints help maintain alignment.
3. **Rehabilitation**:
    1. Start physical therapy early (weeks 2–6) to regain strength and flexibility.
    2. Exercises include isometric ankle presses, heel raises, and balance training.
    3. Avoid rushing; overloading the tendon can cause setbacks.
4. **Pain and Swelling**:
    1. Keep your leg elevated and use ice during the first few days.
    2. Take prescribed pain medications or over-the-counter options like ibuprofen.
5. **Transitioning to Normal Walking**:
    1. Around week 10, switch to supportive shoes with a slight heel lift.
    2. Focus on proper gait mechanics: heel-to-toe motion and even weight distribution.

### Quick Overview:

| ----------- | ------------ | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Phase 1** | Weeks 1–2    | No weight (surgical), use crutches, manage swelling |
| **Phase 2** | Weeks 3–6    | Gradual weight-bearing, start therapy               |
| **Phase 3** | Weeks 7–10   | Walk in boot, reduce heel lifts                     |
| **Phase 4** | Week 10+     | Transition to regular shoes, strengthen             |


**Key Tip**: Follow your surgeon’s advice and listen to your body. Recovery is a step-by-step process - don’t rush it!

## Phase 1: First 2 Weeks After Surgery


The first two weeks following Achilles rupture surgery are crucial for giving your tendon the best chance to heal before introducing weight-bearing activities. During this time, it’s essential to carefully follow your surgeon’s instructions.


### No Weight on the Affected Leg


Avoid putting any weight on your surgical leg during this period. Typically, this involves **touchdown weight-bearing (TDWB)**, where your foot lightly touches the ground while using crutches for support. Make sure your crutches are properly adjusted to your height for safe use. To make things easier at home, set up a comfortable spot - preferably on the main floor - with your leg elevated. Keep essentials like water, medications, and books or devices within easy reach to minimize movement.


### Wearing Protective Boots and Splints


After surgery, your foot will likely be placed in a plaster cast or a half-cast (backslab) to keep it in the correct position [[6]](https://complete-physio.co.uk/vacoped-versus-aircast-boot-what-is-the-best-walking-boot-following-an-achilles-rupture). This cast holds your ankle in a plantarflexed position (toes pointing downward at a 20–30 degree angle), which helps reduce tension on the healing tendon [[3]](https://www.stonybrookmedicine.edu/northforkorthopedics/achilles-tendon).


After the initial cast, you’ll transition to a walking boot. Two common options are:

- **Vacoped Boot**: Costs around $40350 and provides excellent ankle positioning with a pressure-free bead liner.
- **Aircast Boot**: Costs approximately $225 (including wedges) and is widely available, though its ankle support is less refined.

For night-time, an Achilles Tendon Rupture Night Splint (about $89.99) can help maintain proper foot alignment while you sleep. However, avoid walking or standing while wearing the night splint, and wait at least two weeks post-surgery before using one to ensure your wound has finishstarted healing [[7]](https://thetismedical.com/night-splint)[[8]](https://www.amazon.com/Achilles-Orthopaedic-Comfortable-Lightweight-Alternative/dp/B09XLFCJ9F).


### Controlling Pain and Swelling


Managing pain and swelling is key to a smooth recovery. Keep your leg elevated above your heart using pillows or a recliner - propping it on a coffee table won’t be enough to reduce swelling effectively [[4]](https://www.orthopedicandfracturespecialists.com/post-op-achilles-tendon-repair.html).


During the first 3–4 days, icing is especially important. Apply ice for 30 minutes on and 30 minutes off, always using a towel as a barrier to protect your skin [[4]](https://www.orthopedicandfracturespecialists.com/post-op-achilles-tendon-repair.html)[[5]](https://www.orthovirginia.com/our-doctors/thompson/post-operative-instructions). If you have had surgery and are in a cast, ice is still useful.


Your surgeon will likely prescribe pain medications, which may include analgesnarcotics and anti-inflammatory drugs. Take these exactly as instructed. If you don’t have any conditions like kidney or stomach issues, over-the-counter options such as ibuprofen or Aleve can also help with pain and swelling [[4]](https://www.orthopedicandfracturespecialists.com/post-op-achilles-tendon-repair.html)[[5]](https://www.orthovirginia.com/our-doctors/thompson/post-operative-instructions).


Keep the surgical site clean and dry at all times [[3]](https://www.stonybrookmedicine.edu/northforkorthopedics/achilles-tendon). Use a waterproof cover when showering to prevent water from reaching the cast or boot. If you notice increased redness, warmth, or unusual drainage, contact your surgeon immediately, as these could be signs of infection.


Pain medications can sometimes cause constipation, so increase your fibreer intake and drink plenty of water to counteract this side effect [[5]](https://www.orthovirginia.com/our-doctors/thompson/post-operative-instructions).


## Phase 2: Weeks 3-6 - Starting to Bear Weight


Between weeks 3 and 6, you’ll move from protecting your healing tendon to gradually placing weight on your injuredsurgical leg. This phase marks a major step in your recovery, so it’s essential to pay attention to your body and stick to your surgeon’s advice. This period bridges the initial tendon protection phase with the start of active rehabilitation.


### Gradual Weight-Bearing Steps


Around week 3, you can begin partial weight-bearing (PWB), building up at up to 1050% of your body weight while wearing your protective boot [[9]](https://www.stoneclinic.com/achilles-tendon-repair-rehab-protocol). For instance, if you weigh 180 pounds, that’s about 90 pounds of weight initially, increasing quickly.


In weeks 3–4, focus on toe-touchdown weight-bearing with crutches, allowing only light ground contact [[3]](https://www.stonybrookmedicine.edu/northforkorthopedics/achilles-tendon). As you progress into weeks 4–6, you can increase weight-bearing as tolerated, still using both your boot and crutches [[3]](https://www.stonybrookmedicine.edu/northforkorthopedics/achilles-tendon). By the end of week 35, many people are able to achieve full weight-bearing (FWB) while continuing to wear the boot [[9]](https://www.stoneclinic.com/achilles-tendon-repair-rehab-protocol). Expect some mild discomfort and swelling as your body adjusts to the increased load - this is normal [[10]](https://sportsfootankle.com/achilles-crutch-and-boot-weaning-protocol-vail-aspen-denver-co).


Physical therapy typically begins during this phase, with sessions scheduled one to two times per week between weeks 2 and 6 and 8 [[3]](https://www.stonybrookmedicine.edu/northforkorthopedics/achilles-tendon). These early rehab exercises set the foundation for more active recovery in the weeks ahead.


### Adjusting Your Boot with Heel Lifts


As you increase weight-bearing, adjusting your boot is crucial for maintaining proper alignment and safety. During weeks 53–106, the boot is gradually adjusted to bring your ankle closer to its normal position. At 3 weeks post-surgery, the boot is usually set at 30 degrees of plantar flexion, reducing by 510 degrees each week from week 5 [[9]](https://www.stoneclinic.com/achilles-tendon-repair-rehab-protocol).

| Weeks 5-103–4 | 510° less plantarflexion each week | Touchdown with crutchesFull weight               | Reducinged angle         |

By weeks 4–6, if you’re comfortable reaching neutral plantarflexion/dorsiflexion, you can transition to a neutral boot with small heel lifts [[3]](https://www.stonybrookmedicine.edu/northforkorthopedics/achilles-tendon). Keep your boot on 24 hours a day during yourthe early part of this phase. Remove the sole overnight, but make sure to put the boot back on each morning [[9]](https://www.stoneclinic.com/achilles-tendon-repair-rehab-protocol). By week 6, some patients may be able to sleep without the boot, depending on individual progress [[9]](https://www.stoneclinic.com/achilles-tendon-repair-rehab-protocol).

### Basic Strengthening Exercises.


To maintain muscle strength, introduce controlled exercises like isometrics, resistance band work, and seated heel raises. Perform 2–3 sets of 10–25 repetitions or 15–45 second holds, depending on your tolerance. Always move slowly and stop immediately if you experience pain or swelling near the Achilles tendon [[12]](https://www.verywellhealth.com/pt-exercises-for-achilles-tendon-tear-4165931).

- **Isometric ankle exercises:** With your boot on, press the ball of your foot into the ground at a manageable effort. Aim for 2–3 sets of 15–45 second holds, 1–3 times daily [[11]](https://e3rehab.com/achilles-tendon-rupture-rehab).
- **Resistance band exercises:** Use a light resistance band around the ball of your foot for ankle plantar flexion exercises, avoiding dorsiflexion. Perform 2–3 sets of 10–25 repetitions, 1–3 times daily [[11]](https://e3rehab.com/achilles-tendon-rupture-rehab).
- **Seated heel raises:** Work on calf strength by lifting your heel as high as possible and squeezing your calf muscles at the top. Start with your heel elevated on a wedge to limit dorsiflexion, aiming for 2–3 sets of 10–25 repetitions, 1–3 times daily [[11]](https://e3rehab.com/achilles-tendon-rupture-rehab).

Additional exercises, such as towel curls and the short foot exercise, can help minimize muscle atrophy [[11]](https://e3rehab.com/achilles-tendon-rupture-rehab). Once you’re cleared for full weight-bearing, you can also add lower-body exercises like leg extensions, hamstring curls, and modified squats (e.g., squats to a box) while keeping your boot on [[11]](https://e3rehab.com/achilles-tendon-rupture-rehab).


“Exercise gets things moving. Plus, exercise puts you in control and can have the long-term benefit of preventing future problems with your Achilles tendon.”

- Brett Sears, PT [[12]](https://www.verywellhealth.com/pt-exercises-for-achilles-tendon-tear-4165931)

Single-leg balance exercises can also help rebuild proprioception. Start by standing on your uninjured leg on a flat surface, aiming for 3 sets of 60 seconds. Use your hands for support if needed, and gradually build confidence and strength [[11]](https://e3rehab.com/achilles-tendon-rupture-rehab).

## Phase 3: Weeks 7-10 - Learning to Walk in the Boot


Weeks 7 through 10 mark an important stage in your recovery journey. This is the time to focus on refining your walking technique while still using the boot, setting the stage for a smooth transition to regular footwear. The key here is to work on proper gait mechanics to avoid any setbacks.


### How to Walk Properly in Your Boot


Walking correctly in your boot is essential for rebuilding strength and balance. The goal is to develop a natural walking pattern - starting with your heel, rolling through the foot, and pushing off with your toes [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time). To ensure even weight distribution, consider using a mirror to check your alignment and posture while practicing.


During this phase, the heel wedge in your boot is gradually reduced. By week 7, the wedge is typically lowered to about a quarter inch (0.5 cm) [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time). Since the boot adds height, it’s a good idea to use an [Evenup](https://opedmedical.com/product/evenup-shoe-lift/?srsltid=AfmBOoq2xb9d18mA6oZEM7CXY30gA2EK6qERHNxd7WS2VAvkDdwHBeRu) shoe leveller and wear a running shoe with a thick sole on your uninjured foot to maintain proper alignment [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time). If stability is an issue, use a balance aid until you feel more secure [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time).


### Stopping Boot Use Gradually


Transitioning out of the boot requires careful planning. Between weeks 108 and 12, you may start wearing regular shoes fitted with a quarter-inch (0.5 cm) heel wedge [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time). When choosing shoes for this phase, look for ones with at least a 10 mm heel-to-toe drop to maintain the necessary heel lift [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time). Always follow your healthcare provider’s advice as you gradually reduce your reliance on the boot and heel lifts.


It’s normal to experience some discomfort or swelling as you progress [[10]](https://sportsfootankle.com/achilles-crutch-and-boot-weaning-protocol-vail-aspen-denver-co). However, if the pain or swelling becomes significant, it’s important to slow down and consult your healthcare provider before continuing.


### Advanced Exercises for Strength and Movement


As your recovery advances, the focus shifts to improving your gait, strengthening your ankle’s plantar flexors, and gradually increasing ankle dorsiflexion [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499). Once you’re fully weight-bearing in the CAM boot, gait training in an athletic shoe with heel lifts can begin [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499).


If you’re dealing with gait issues like a prolonged stance or a weak push-off, targeted exercises can help retrain those movements. Practicing the heel-to-toe walking motion is especially effective [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499). Other helpful options include aquatic therapy and unweighted treadmills, as these provide support while allowing for more natural movement [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499).


Keep in mind that during the gait cycle, your Achilles tendon experiences loads of 2–4 times your body weight [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499). Pay close attention to any signs of stiffness, pain, or swelling, and adjust your activities accordingly. Understanding how to manage load progression and listening to your body are critical to avoiding over-exertion.


This phase may also include advanced strengthening exercises aimed at enhancing balance and improving ankle function. Always work closely with your physical therapist when introducing more challenging activities. Monitoring your symptoms and following professional guidance will help ensure a safe and steady recovery.


## Phase 4: Week 10 and Beyond - Walking Without Support


You’ve made it to the final stage of your walking recovery journey. At this point, you’ll leave behind protective boots and heel lifts as you work toward walking independently. While the timeline varies, many people reach this stage between weeks 10 and 12 after surgery. It’s also time to adjust your footwear and prepare for life beyond supportive boots.


### Switching to Regular Shoes


The shift from boots to regular shoes is a gradual process. Around week 108, you’ll start with supportive footwear that includes a single gel heel pad. By week 160, you’ll begin reducing or removing heel lifts, depending on your comfort and progress.


When choosing your first regular shoes, look for ones with at least a 10 mm heel-to-toe drop. This slight elevation helps ease the transition and reduces strain on your recovering tendon. Running shoes or athletic footwear are excellent choices because they offer cushioning and support. Avoid entirely flat shoes or bare feet at this stage, as they can put unnecessary pressure on your Achilles tendon.


### Walking Normally Again


After weeks of modified walking, regaining a natural stride takes some effort. Your goal is to restore the heel-to-toe motion: landing on your heel, rolling through your foot, and pushing off with your toes. This may feel awkward at first, but deliberate practice will help.


Using a mirror can be a helpful tool. Watch your alignment and ensure both legs are bearing weight evenly. It’s common to develop a limp or favour your uninjured leg, so concentrate on taking balanced steps and spending equal time on each foot.


Start on flat surfaces and gradually try walking on different terrains, such as grass, gravel, or gentle slopes. These variations challenge your balance and build strength. Pay attention to your body - don’t push through significant pain or discomfort.


If a limp persists or push-off feels difficult, targeted exercises can make a difference. Practice the heel-to-toe motion slowly, focusing on proper mechanics rather than speed. Aquatic therapy can also be a great option, as water supports your body while allowing for more natural movement.


As your walking pattern improves, it’s time to focus on strengthening your Achilles and surrounding muscles.


### Exercises to Keep Your Achilles Strong


To support your recovery, your exercise routine should aim to build strength, improve balance, and enhance coordination.

- **Standing Heel Raises:** These are key to rebuilding calf strength. Start with equal weight on both legs, and as you get stronger, shift more weight to your recovering leg. Push through your big toe (first metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint) for proper muscle activation.
- **Seated Calf Raises:** Use light resistance to build endurance in your calf muscles. Focus on controlled movements rather than heavy loads.
- **Balance Exercises:** Simple activities like tandem stance or single-leg balance help retrain your proprioception - your sense of body position and movement. Begin with a wall for support and progress to balancing unassisted.
- **Eccentric Calf Drops:** These exercises, where the muscle lengthens under tension, are especially effective for strengthening the tendon. Start slowly and increase repetitions as you gain confidence. Usually not advised until 6 months.

“Active stretches strengthen the tendon and calf while it lengthens them, whereas passive stretches only lengthen them.” [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time) - Maryke Louw, Chartered Physiotherapist


Don’t forget the rest of your leg muscles. Strengthening your glutes, hamstrings, and quadriceps helps maintain proper walking mechanics and prevents injuries caused by overcompensation.


If you’re planning to return to sports, plyometric exercises like hops and jumps can be introduced later in your recovery. For now, prioritize mastering basic walking and strengthening exercises.


Low-impact activities such as stationary biking can help maintain your cardio fitness without straining your tendon. Swimming is another excellent option once your incision is fully healed and your doctor gives the green light for water activities.


Reaching the point where you can walk without support is a huge achievement, but recovery doesn’t stop here. Consistency with exercises, listening to your body, and staying in touch with your healthcare team will keep you on track toward full recovery.


## Key Points for Walking Again After Achilles Surgery


Recovering from an Achilles rupture is a journey that requires patience and a well-structured plan. Research shows that complete recovery can take a year or longer [[14]](https://thetismedical.com/FAQs/life-after-achilles-rupture), even though many rehabilitation programs aim for a return to sports around the six-month mark [[1]](https://thetismedical.com/FAQs/achilles-rupture-timeline). Knowing what to expect at each stage helps you stay focused and avoid potential setbacks.


Recovery moves through specific phases, starting with strict non-weight bearing, transitioning to gradual loading, then boot-assisted walking, and finally independent movement. The later stages - typically from week 13 to week 25 - emphasize building strength and increasing activity levels [[1]](https://thetismedical.com/FAQs/achilles-rupture-timeline).


Wearing a boot for about 10 weeks is crucial for protecting the tendon and ensuring proper alignment. Skipping steps or rushing through the timeline can lead to complications like tendon elongation or even re-rupture. It’s worth noting that the most significant risk of tendon elongation occurs between 2 and 6 weeks after surgery [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499).


A fully healed Achilles tendon is incredibly strong, capable of handling loads up to 11 times your body weight during high-speed running [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499). However, during recovery, it’s essential to respect certain restrictions. For instance, avoid dorsiflexion beyond neutral for the first 810 weeks and hold off on strong passive calf stretching until at least 612 monthweeks post-surgery to safeguard the repair [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499).


Your recovery timeline will depend on factors like your age, overall health, and the severity of your injury [[1]](https://thetismedical.com/FAQs/achilles-rupture-timeline). While these timelines provide a general guide, the most important thing is to monitor your symptoms and progression closely. Avoid rushing through the phases, trust the process, and remain consistent with your exercises. Every small step forward brings you closer to walking independently again.


## FAQs


### **What should I consider when choosing a walking boot after Achilles rupture surgery?**


When choosing a walking boot after Achilles rupture surgery, prioritize support, comfort, adjustability, and how well it aligns with your recovery phase. A well-designed boot usually includes a sturdy outer shell for protection, a cushioned interior for comfort, and adjustable straps to accommodate swelling. Some models also feature a curved sole to encourage a more natural walking motion while easing strain on your tendon. Options like the Vacoped or Aircast boots come with specific advantages. The Vacoped, for instance, offers controlled ankle movement and can be adjusted as you progress through recovery. Always stick to your healthcare provider's recommendations regarding weight-bearing and boot usage to ensure a smooth and safe healing process.


### **What's the best way to manage pain and swelling after Achilles rupture surgery?**


To help manage pain and swelling after Achilles rupture surgery, focus on keeping your leg elevated and using ice regularly. Elevating your leg above heart level - especially during the first few days - can significantly reduce swelling and ease discomfort. Use pillows to support your leg while you rest or sleep. For icing, apply an ice pack for 20–30 minutes every 2 hours. Always wrap the ice pack in a thin cloth to protect your skin. Over-the-counter pain relievers like ibuprofen can help with mild discomfort, but your doctor might prescribe stronger medications if needed during the early recovery period. It's crucial to follow your surgeon's post-operative care instructions closely. If you're ever unsure or have concerns about managing pain or swelling, don't hesitate to contact your healthcare provider.


### **What are the best exercises to regain balance and strength after transitioning out of a walking boot?**


To rebuild balance and strength after transitioning out of a walking boot following Achilles rupture surgery, it's important to focus on exercises that target stability, coordination, and muscle rebuilding. Start with single-leg balance exercises: stand on your recovering leg for 30 seconds, and as your stability improves, gradually increase the duration. Incorporating calf raises is another key step - begin with both legs, then progress to single-leg raises to strengthen the calf muscles effectively. For improving coordination and balance, try heel-to-toe walking in a straight line. Perform these exercises with caution, and whenever possible, work with a physical therapist to ensure proper technique and to avoid placing too much strain on your healing tendon. Always stick to your doctor or therapist's guidance for a safe and steady recovery.

### Wearing Protective Boots and Splints


After surgery, your foot will likely be placed in a plaster cast or a half-cast (backslab) to keep it in the correct position [[6]](https://complete-physio.co.uk/vacoped-versus-aircast-boot-what-is-the-best-walking-boot-following-an-achilles-rupture). This cast holds your ankle in a plantarflexed position (toes pointing downward at a 20–30 degree angle), which helps reduce tension on the healing tendon [[3]](https://www.stonybrookmedicine.edu/northforkorthopedics/achilles-tendon).


After the initial cast, you’ll transition to a walking boot. Two common options are:

- **Vacoped Boot**: Costs around $400 and provides excellent ankle positioning with a pressure-free bead liner.
- **Aircast Boot**: Costs approximately $225 (including wedges) and is widely available, though its ankle support is less refined.

| Vacoped       | $400     | Excellent         | More equinus          | Limited          |

However, avoid walking or standing while wearing the night splint, and wait at least two weeks post-surgery before using one to ensure your wound has finished healing [[7]](https://thetismedical.com/night-splint)[[8]](https://www.amazon.com/Achilles-Orthopaedic-Comfortable-Lightweight-Alternative/dp/B09XLFCJ9F).


### Controlling Pain and Swelling


Managing pain and swelling is key to a smooth recovery. Keep your leg elevated above your heart using pillows or a recliner - propping it on a coffee table won’t be enough to reduce swelling effectively [[4]](https://www.orthopedicandfracturespecialists.com/post-op-achilles-tendon-repair.html).


During the first 3–4 days, icing is especially important. Apply ice for 30 minutes on and 30 minutes off, always using a towel as a barrier to protect your skin [[4]](https://www.orthopedicandfracturespecialists.com/post-op-achilles-tendon-repair.html)[[5]](https://www.orthovirginia.com/our-doctors/thompson/post-operative-instructions). If you have had surgery and are in a cast, ice is still useful.


Your surgeon will likely prescribe pain medications, which may include analgesics and anti-inflammatory drugs. Take these exactly as instructed. If you don’t have any conditions like kidney or stomach issues, over-the-counter options such as ibuprofen or Aleve can also help with pain and swelling [[4]](https://www.orthopedicandfracturespecialists.com/post-op-achilles-tendon-repair.html)[[5]](https://www.orthovirginia.com/our-doctors/thompson/post-operative-instructions).


## Phase 2: Weeks 3-6 - Starting to Bear Weight


Between weeks 3 and 6, you’ll move from protecting your healing tendon to gradually placing weight on your injured leg. This phase marks a major step in your recovery, so it’s essential to pay attention to your body and stick to your surgeon’s advice. This period bridges the initial tendon protection phase with the start of active rehabilitation.


### Gradual Weight-Bearing Steps


Around week 3, you can begin partial weight-bearing (PWB), building up to 10% of your body weight while wearing your protective boot [[9]](https://www.stoneclinic.com/achilles-tendon-repair-rehab-protocol). For instance, if you weigh 180 pounds, that’s about 90 pounds of weight initially, increasing quickly.


By the end of week 3, many people are able to achieve full weight-bearing (FWB) while continuing to wear the boot [9]. Expect some mild discomfort and swelling as your body adjusts to the increased load - this is normal [10].


Physical therapy typically begins during this phase, with sessions scheduled one to two times per week between weeks 6 and 8 [[3]](https://www.stonybrookmedicine.edu/northforkorthopedics/achilles-tendon). These early rehab exercises set the foundation for more active recovery in the weeks ahead.


### Adjusting Your Boot with Heel Lifts


As you increase weight-bearing, adjusting your boot is crucial for maintaining proper alignment and safety. During weeks 5–10, the boot is gradually adjusted to bring your ankle closer to its normal position. At 3 weeks post-surgery, the boot is usually set at 30 degrees of plantar flexion, reducing by 5 degrees each week from week 5 [[9]](https://www.stoneclinic.com/achilles-tendon-repair-rehab-protocol).


| **Week**  | **Boot Angle**                   | **Weight-Bearing Status**                        | **Key Changes**          |
| --------- | -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------ |
| Weeks 3–5 | 20° plantarflexion               | Touchdown with crutches; build up to full weight | Initial boot positioning |
| Week 5-10 | 5° less plantarflexion each week | Full weight                                      | Reducing angle           |
| Week 10   | Neutral with heel lifts          | Weight-bearing                                   | Near-normal positioning  |


Keep your boot on 24 hours a day during your Recovery, unless you choose to use the Night Splint (Thetis Medical) for resting. This device is comfortable, but not designed for walking in.


During this phase, the heel wedge in your boot is gradually reduced. By week 7, the wedge is typically lowered to about a quarter inch (0.5 cm) [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time). Since the boot adds height, it’s a good idea to use an [Evenup](https://opedmedical.com/product/evenup-shoe-lift/?srsltid=AfmBOoq2xb9d18mA6oZEM7CXY30gA2EK6qERHNxd7WS2VAvkDdwHBeRu) shoe leveller and wear a running shoe with a thick sole on your uninjured foot to maintain proper alignment [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time). If stability is an issue, use a balance aid until you feel more secure [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time).


### Stopping Boot Use Gradually


Transitioning out of the boot requires careful planning. Between weeks 10 and 12, you may start wearing regular shoes fitted with a quarter-inch (0.5 cm) heel wedge [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time). When choosing shoes for this phase, look for ones with at least a 10 mm heel-to-toe drop to maintain the necessary heel lift [[13]](https://www.treatmyachilles.com/post/achilles-rehab-after-surgery-exercises-targets-and-recovery-time). Always follow your healthcare provider’s advice as you gradually reduce your reliance on the boot and heel lift


As your recovery advances, the focus shifts to improving your gait, strengthening your ankle’s plantar flexors, and gradually increasing ankle dorsiflexion [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499). Once you’re fully weight-bearing in the boot, gait training in an athletic shoe with heel lifts can begin [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499).


Keep in mind that during the gait cycle, your Achilles tendon experiences loads of 2–4 times your body weight [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499). Pay close attention to any signs of stiffness, pain, or swelling, and adjust your activities accordingly. Understanding how to manage load progression and listening to your body are critical to avoiding over-exertion.



### Switching to Regular Shoes


The shift from boots to regular shoes is a gradual process. Around week 10, you’ll start with supportive footwear that includes a single gel heel pad. By week 16, you’ll begin reducing or removing heel lifts, depending on your comfort and progress.


When choosing your first regular shoes, look for ones with at least a 10 mm heel-to-toe drop. This slight elevation helps ease the transition and reduces strain on your recovering tendon. Running shoes or athletic footwear are excellent choices because they offer cushioning and support. Avoid entirely flat shoes or bare feet at this stage, as they can put unnecessary pressure on your Achilles tendon.


Using a mirror can be a helpful tool. Watch your alignment and ensure both legs are bearing weight evenly. It’s common to develop a limp or favour your uninjured leg, so concentrate on taking balanced steps and spending equal time on each foot.



• **Standing Heel Raises:** These are key to rebuilding calf strength. Start with equal weight on both legs, and as you get stronger, shift more weight to your recovering leg. Push through your big toe (first metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint) for proper muscle activation.



• **Eccentric Calf Drops:** These exercises, where the muscle lengthens under tension, are especially effective for strengthening the tendon. Start slowly and increase repetitions as you gain confidence. Usually not advised until 6 months.


A fully healed Achilles tendon is incredibly strong, capable of handling loads up to 11 times your body weight during high-speed running [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499). However, during recovery, it’s essential to respect certain restrictions. For instance, avoid dorsiflexion beyond neutral for the first 10 weeks and hold off on strong passive calf stretching until at least 6 months post-surgery to safeguard the repair [[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499).
