**Phase 1 (Weeks 0–2): Rest and Protection**


During this critical period, your foot will be immobilized in a plaster cast or a rigid boot set at 20-30 degrees of plantarflexion (toes pointing downward). Weight-bearing is initially limitedoff-limits to allow the skin (in surgical cases) and tendon to start healing. Pain management and reducing the initial trauma are the primary goals here [[5]](https://www.londonorthopaedicsurgery.co.uk/therapies-exercise-regimes/achilles-tendon-rupture-non-operative-treatment-rehabilitation-guidelines)[[4]](https://thetismedical.com/FAQs/achilles-rupture-timeline).


**Phase 2 (Weeks 2–4): Easy Walking**


At this stage, you’ll switch to a rigid walking boot with hinge/wedges and begin partial weight-bearing with the help of crutches, as tolerated. Gentle plantarflexion exercises may be introduced to promote movement while still protecting the tendon. It’s also during this phase that you and your specialist may make a final decisiondecide between surgical and non-surgical treatment options [[5]](https://www.londonorthopaedicsurgery.co.uk/therapies-exercise-regimes/achilles-tendon-rupture-non-operative-treatment-rehabilitation-guidelines)[[4]](https://thetismedical.com/FAQs/achilles-rupture-timeline).



**Phase 3 (Weeks 4–8): Walking**


This is whenBy now you should be full weight-bearing begins, although you might still use crutches for balance. The boot stays on, but the wedges are gradually removed/hinge angle adjusted as your ankle regains mobility. Physical therapy typically starts at the end of this stagehere, focusing on gentle range-of-motion exercises to ease your way into more active movement [[5]](https://www.londonorthopaedicsurgery.co.uk/therapies-exercise-regimes/achilles-tendon-rupture-non-operative-treatment-rehabilitation-guidelines)[[4]](https://thetismedical.com/FAQs/achilles-rupture-timeline).



**Phase 5 (Weeks 12–24): Active Recovery**


The focus here is on progressive strengthening and improving proprioception (your sense of body position). You’ll work on achieving a normal dorsiflexion range and may even start light jogging. By now, the boot is completely replaced with regular shoes that provide heel lift and support [[5]](https://www.londonorthopaedicsurgery.co.uk/therapies-exercise-regimes/achilles-tendon-rupture-non-operative-treatment-rehabilitation-guidelines)[[4]](https://thetismedical.com/FAQs/achilles-rupture-timeline).


**Phase 6 (Week 24+): Full Activity**


At this point, you’re working toward full recovery and returning to regular activities. Normal footwear is back in play, and if you’re an athlete, sport-specific training can begin. However, keep in mind that up to 46% of people who experience an Achilles tendon rupture have a history of tendon pain or tendinopathy


[[2]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11379499) . This makes ongoing monitoring essential, even during this final phase.


Here’s a quick summary of the six recovery phases:

| Rest         | 0–2 weeks     | Protect repair, manage pain | Non-weight bearing if surgery         |
| Easy Walking | 2–4 weeks     | Begin gentle movement       | WPartial weight bearing with crutches |
| Walking      | 4–8 weeks     | Restore walking pattern     | Full weight bearing                   |

| Full         | 24+ weeks     | Return to sport/activity    | Unrestricted                          |


**How to perform:**


Sit on the floor with your injured leg extended straight. Wrap a towel around the ball of your foot, holding both ends firmly. Gently pull the towel toward you until you feel a _mild_ stretch in your calf and Achilles tendon. Hold this position for 30 seconds, then relax.


### 2. Runner’s Stretch for the un-injured leg


This standing stretch targets your calf muscles and Achilles tendon, preparing them for more demanding activities as you progress.


**When to start:** Typically introduced 16–20 weeks post-injury,_**Only**_ with your healthcare specialists approval, once you’ve mastered basic stretches and have good balance.



**How to perform:**


Stand on a step with the ball of your injured foot on the edge and your heel hanging off. Hold onto a railing for support. Slowly rise onto tip toes and then lower your heels just below the step until you feel a mild stretch in your calf and Achilles tendon. Hold for 30 seconds, then raise your heel back up.

The first 12 weeks are particularly crucial. During this period, it’s critical to keep your protective boot on  (to maintain a tip toe posture) unless your provider says otherwise. Walking without support too soon can lead to a re-rupture, potentially requiring additional surgery and delaying your recovery. Beyond boot use, pay close attention to what your body is telling you.



**Pay attention to your body’s signals.** Pain levels can guide you in determining how much activity is appropriate. Avoid any movements that cause even moderate to severe discomfort [[12]](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7249277). If you experience sudden cramps in your calf, thigh, or groin, or notice increased swelling in your leg, ankle, or foot, contact your healthcare provider immediately [[6]](https://www.cuh.nhs.uk/patient-information/achilles-tendon-rupture-management-and-rehabilitation)[[14]](https://medlineplus.gov/ency/patientinstructions/000546.htm).
