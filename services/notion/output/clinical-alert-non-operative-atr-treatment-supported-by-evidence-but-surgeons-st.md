## **🚨 Clinical Alert: Non-Operative ATR Treatment Supported by Evidence, But Surgeons Still Choose the Knife** 🔪


Despite strong evidence favoring a shift towards non-operative management for Acute Achilles Tendon Rupture (**ATR**), a recent analysis highlights a significant gap between current literature and surgical practice.


### **Key Details: The Evidence-Practice Disconnect** 📉

- 📅 **Published:** 2023 (Canadian Journal of Surgery - _Western Authors: Cohen, Sandman, et al._)
- **Evidence Base:** The current literature **clearly supports non-operative management** as the primary treatment pathway for most ATR injuries.
- **Practice:** Many surgeons continue to treat these injuries **operatively**, suggesting a non-adherence to the latest evidence-based guidelines.
- **Exceptions:** The evidence suggests that **Achilles insertional tears** and **athletic patients** are two groups where the benefits of operative intervention may still warrant further research or preference.

### **Clinical Insight** 💡


This post emphasizes the need for conformity in the surgical and rehabilitation community. While non-operative protocols using **early functional rehabilitation** have dramatically improved outcomes, the decision-making process must be transparent and address the specific risks and demands of the patient. Understanding why surgeons may still prefer surgery—especially for high-demand athletes—is key to promoting better, standardized, evidence-based care.


**Question:** In your experience, what is the single biggest factor preventing surgeons from fully adopting non-operative treatment protocols for the majority of ATR cases?
