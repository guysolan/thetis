## [Facebook: The Community Guide]


**The sound you can’t "un-hear": That unmistakable Achilles snap.**


Most patients describe the moment of rupture with haunting similarity: a loud "crack" like a whip, followed by the immediate sensation that someone has kicked them squarely in the heel. When you turn around and find no one there, the reality sets in. That "snap" is the sound of the strongest tendon in your body losing its tension, and the first 24 hours that follow are the most critical for your future mobility.


In these early hours, the goal isn't progress—it’s preservation. When the tendon ruptures, the two ends begin to retract away from each other. **The primary clinical objective is "approximation," which means bringing those retracted ends back together by immediately placing the foot in a toes-down (plantar-flexed) position.**


Whether you are heading for surgery or non-operative management, failing to immobilise the foot in this position early can lead to the tendon healing in a "lengthened" state. A long tendon is a weak tendon, often resulting in a permanent loss of power when you eventually try to push off or jump. ⏱️


For those who have been through it: how soon after that "snap" were you placed in a boot or a cast?
