## Content overview


### Revised Post


**Night pain and poor sleep are common in the early phase after Achilles rupture.**


In many patients, this is caused by **poor night positioning**, not the injury itself.


Here is what clinicians can address immediately:


🛏️ **Position the foot in gentle plantarflexion overnight**


Avoid leaving the foot unsupported or hanging into dorsiflexion, which increases passive tendon strain.


🦶 **Ensure the heel is fully offloaded during sleep**


Continuous heel contact can increase pressure and night pain, particularly in casts or walking boots.


🔁 **Use a removable night splint instead of a boot when appropriate**


This allows controlled positioning while improving comfort and sleep tolerance.


📅 **Actively check night positioning at early follow up visits**


Many patients adjust their position at night without realising it worsens symptoms.


Poor sleep negatively affects pain perception, rehabilitation engagement, and overall recovery.


Night positioning is a small detail, but one that can be corrected easily and early.


Do you routinely address night positioning when counselling Achilles rupture patients?

Night pain and poor sleep are among the most common complaints after Achilles rupture.


Much of it comes down to **uncontrolled plantarflexion and heel pressure overnight** 🌙🦶


For patients in early recovery, a few small adjustments can make a big difference:


🛏️ **Maintain gentle plantarflexion at night**


Reduces passive tendon strain compared with a neutral or dangling foot position


🦶 **Offload the heel completely**


Continuous heel contact increases focal pressure and night pain, especially in casts or boots


🔁 **Use a removable night splint rather than a boot when appropriate**


Allows controlled positioning while improving comfort and sleep adherence


📅 **Reinforce night positioning during early rehab reviews**


Patients often self adjust at night without realising the impact on symptoms


Poor sleep affects pain perception, rehab engagement, and overall recovery. Night time positioning is a small detail but one that clinicians can easily optimise.


**Clinical question:** How much attention do you give to night positioning when counselling Achilles rupture patients?
