The biggest risk to your recovery isn’t moving too slowly—it’s "healing long.”


A healthy Achilles tendon is remarkably stiff; it acts like a high-tension spring that resists stretching even under heavy loads. However, in the early weeks following a rupture, the newly formed tissue is highly "compliant." This means it is soft, vulnerable, and stretches far too easily under pressure.


Moving the foot into a "neutral" 90-degree position too soon can cause the tendon to permanently elongate. A tendon that heals too long loses its natural "snap," which significantly weakens the ability to push off when walking or running later in the recovery journey.


Specific bracing and gradual loading protocols exist to prevent this excessive elongation while the tissue matures. By **maintaining a shortened position with heel wedges**, collagen fibres are able to knit together more densely, eventually restoring that vital clinical stiffness required for sport.


Have you found it difficult to balance the desire to move more with the biological need to protect the length of the tendon? 🦶
