Surgical Fix for a Ruptured Achilles: Necessary or Outdated?


Fifteen years ago, a landmark study by Willits et al. challenged the consensus on Achilles rupture treatment, proving that surgery might be unnecessary. The research showed that with an accelerated rehab protocol, nonoperative care could deliver similar outcomes with fewer complications. Yet, the debate rages on, and treatment choices still vary wildly by country.


Key findings from a total of 144 patients:

- **Re-rupture Rate:** Only **2.8%** in the operative group vs. **4.2%** nonoperative. A small, statistically insignificant difference. 📈
- **Complications:** A staggering **18.1%** complication rate in the operative group, more than double the **8.3%** in the nonoperative group. 🤕
- **Functional Outcomes:** Patients in both groups had clinically similar strength, range of motion, and calf circumference. 💪

Today, in the UK, most Achilles ruptures are managed nonoperatively, while in the US, the operative approach is still highly favored. Given the data, why do we continue to operate so frequently?
