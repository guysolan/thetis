## Beyond the Boot: Managing Your Clot Risk


An Achilles rupture is more than just a tendon injury; the resulting immobilisation significantly increases your risk of a blood clot, or venous thromboembolism (VTE). When your leg is kept still in a boot, your "calf pump"—the mechanism that helps return blood to your heart—is effectively switched off. This slower blood flow, combined with local inflammation, makes **vigilance just as important as your physical rehab.**


Depending on your specific risk factors and your clinic's protocol, you may be prescribed VTE prophylaxis. This typically involves either daily self-injections, such as enoxaparin, or oral tablets like rivaroxaban or apixaban. While tablets are more convenient, they aren't suitable for every patient. Regardless of the medication, you must know the "Red Flags."


A **DVT (clot in the leg)** often presents as new calf pain, one-leg swelling, warmth, or a feeling of tightness. The greater danger is a **PE (clot in the lungs)**, which is a medical emergency. If you experience sudden breathlessness, chest pain, or fainting, seek help immediately. Awareness is your first line of defence.


Does your current recovery plan include daily medication for clot prevention, or are you following a risk-stratified approach?
