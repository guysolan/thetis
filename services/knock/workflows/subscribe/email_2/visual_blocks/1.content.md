The early days are about one thing: protection. Your tendon needs to heal, and the best way to help it is to keep it shortened and safe.

## The Golden Rule: Keep the Tendon Ends Together

This is the most important thing you need to know right now. Keeping your ankle in a tiptoe position (toes down, what clinicians call plantarflexion) pushes the two ends of the ruptured tendon together. 

Think of recovery like a game of **snakes and ladders**: keeping the ends together moves you forward (ladder), but letting your toes come up pulls the ends apart (snake).

**What this means:**

- **Keep your ankle pointed down 24/7** — don't let your toes come up
- **Assume you are non-weightbearing** unless your clinician specifically told you otherwise
- **Do not test the tendon** or try to stretch it
- **Keep your boot or splint on** unless your clinician explicitly says it's safe to remove it

## Other Day 1 Essentials

- **Elevate your ankle** above heart level as much as possible to reduce swelling
- **Take it slow** — use your crutches and move carefully, especially on stairs and in the bathroom
- **Know the blood clot red flags** (DVT and PE) and seek urgent care if they appear

## When to Get Urgent Help

If you experience any of these, seek urgent care immediately:

- New calf swelling, pain, or tenderness
- Chest pain, breathlessness, or coughing blood
- Numb, blue, or pale toes
- A new "pop" after a fall with sudden worsening pain
- Severe increasing pressure or pain in your cast/boot
