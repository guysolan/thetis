Around week 8, you're likely making good progress and feeling stronger. This is an exciting phase, but it's also when rerupture risk is highest.

## Why Rerupture Risk Is Higher Now

At this stage:

- **Your tendon has healed** but isn't yet fully strong
- **You're moving more** and may feel confident
- **The temptation to push** can be strong
- **But the tendon isn't ready** for high forces yet

This combination makes weeks 8-12 a critical period for preventing rerupture.

## Common Causes of Rerupture

**What to avoid:**

- **Sudden, forceful movements** — jumping, running, or explosive movements
- **Stretching with toes up** — this puts dangerous tension on the healing tendon
- **Removing wedges too early** — this allows the tendon to stretch before it's strong enough
- **Slipping or falling** — be extra careful on wet or uneven surfaces

## Warning Signs to Watch For

**Contact your medical team immediately if you experience:**

- **Sudden, sharp pain** in the back of your ankle or calf
- **A "pop" or "snap" sensation** — especially during activity
- **Sudden inability to push off** with your toes
- **Swelling that increases** after activity
- **Bruising** that appears suddenly
- **Feeling like something "gave way"**

Early intervention can prevent a full rerupture.

## Safe Progression Principles

**Gradual is key:**

- **Progress slowly** — small increases in activity, not big jumps
- **Listen to your body** — if something hurts, stop
- **Avoid high-impact activities** until cleared by your clinician

**The 10% rule:**

- Increase activity by no more than **10% per week**
- This applies to walking distance, exercise intensity, and activity duration

**When in doubt, slow down.** It's better to take an extra week than to risk rerupture.
