# Good carousel references

Gold-standard examples for the ATR Recovery Carousel setting. Structure: **hook → 1 clear answer (2–4 slides) → close**. No boilerplate safety slide — weave warnings into answer slides when relevant.

Each answer slide should carry **2–4 short lines**, not a single sentence.

---

## Shoe transition

**Hook → answer:** How to leave the boot safely — heel lifts, gradual weaning, listen to your body.

**Summary:** Optimising shoe transition after boot removal: heel lifts, gradual weaning, and knowing when to slow down.

### Slide 1 — Hook

- **Headline:** READY TO DITCH THE BOOT?
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Leaving your boot for shoes feels like a big step. Most people get there around weeks 10–12 — but only when your team says you're ready.
- **Visual:** Patient placing a shoe next to the boot, high-key and bright.

### Slide 2 — Answer (name it)

- **Headline:** START WITH HEEL LIFTS
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Use 0.5–1 cm heel lifts in both shoes at first. They reduce strain on the healing tendon while your ankle adjusts to a flatter position.
- **Visual:** Illustration showing shoe with heel lift insert, clinical minimalist style.

### Slide 3 — Answer (how)

- **Headline:** WEAN GRADUALLY — NOT ALL AT ONCE
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Start with a few hours in shoes each day while keeping the boot for the rest. Increase shoe time over 1–2 weeks if pain and swelling stay manageable.
- **Visual:** Simple timeline graphic: boot → boot + shoes → mostly shoes.

### Slide 4 — Answer (what good shoes look like)

- **Headline:** CHOOSE SUPPORT, NOT FLAT SOLES
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** A stiff heel counter and slight heel raise help. Avoid flat slip-ons or barefoot walking until your clinician clears it.
- **Visual:** Supportive trainer next to a flat slip-on, with tick and cross.

### Slide 5 — Answer (caution woven in)

- **Headline:** IF IT FLARES UP, STEP BACK
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** More swelling, sharp pain, limping, or a "giving way" feeling? Slow down and tell your clinician — going back to more boot time for a day or two is normal.
- **Visual:** Patient resting foot on a cushion, calm home setting.

### Final slide — Close

- **Headline:** EVERY STEP IS PROGRESS
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Shoe transition is a milestone, not a race. Small steps forward add up.
- **Visual:** Patient walking outdoors in supportive shoes, confident posture.

## The No.1 question to ask your surgeon

**Hook → answer:** Before surgery, ask "Why not non-operative treatment?"

**Summary:** The question to ask before Achilles surgery, and how non-operative care compares.

### Slide 1 — Hook

- **Headline:** BEFORE YOU CONSENT TO SURGERY, ASK THIS
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Many people assume surgery is the default. It isn't — and you deserve to understand both paths before you decide.
- **Visual:** Patient in clinic, thoughtful expression, surgeon across the desk.

### Slide 2 — Answer (name it)

- **Headline:** "WHY NOT NON-OPERATIVE TREATMENT?"
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** For many people, non-surgical treatment in a boot is first-line. Major trials (including UKSTAR) show outcomes comparable to surgery for most patients.
- **Visual:** Doctor and patient in conversation, open body language.

### Slide 3 — Answer (trade-offs)

- **Headline:** SURGERY HAS DIFFERENT RISKS
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Surgery can mean wound problems, infection, and scar issues. Non-operative care avoids the operating room — but still needs strict boot compliance.
- **Visual:** Clean split graphic: surgical wound care vs boot + crutches.

### Slide 4 — Answer (re-rupture)

- **Headline:** RE-RUPTURE RATES ARE LOW EITHER WAY
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Re-rupture affects roughly 3–5% of people — whether you have surgery or not. Neither path eliminates that risk completely.
- **Visual:** Simple stat callout, calm clinical tone (no alarmist red).

### Slide 5 — Answer (when surgery IS considered)

- **Headline:** WHEN SURGERY MAY BE RECOMMENDED
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** A large tendon gap, certain sports demands, or failed non-operative progress can push the balance toward surgery. Gap size is one factor — your activity level and overall health matter too.
- **Visual:** Surgeon pointing at scan, patient listening — collaborative, not pushy.

### Final slide — Close

- **Headline:** DECIDE WITH YOUR TEAM
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** There is no one-size-fits-all answer. Ask questions, take your time, and choose the path you and your clinician trust.
- **Visual:** Patient walking out of clinic, reassured.

### Captions

**Instagram**

> Before Achilles surgery, ask: "Why not non-operative treatment?" For many patients, boot treatment works as well as surgery — with different risks on each side. Re-rupture rates are low either way (~3–5%). Talk through your situation with your team before deciding.

## Biggest mistake in ATR recovery

**Hook → answer:** The overlooked problem isn't re-rupture — it's tendon elongation (healing too long).

**Summary:** Why elongation matters and how boot protocol and avoiding early aggressive stretching protect tendon length.

### Slide 1 — Hook

- **Headline:** THE MISTAKE MOST ATR PATIENTS NEVER HEAR ABOUT
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Everyone warns you about re-rupture. Almost nobody explains the other problem that can limit your strength for years.
- **Visual:** Patient looking at boot, contemplative, home setting.

### Slide 2 — Answer (name it)

- **Headline:** IT'S TENDON ELONGATION
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Your tendon can heal **long** — in a stretched position. That makes push-off and running harder even if the tendon never snaps again.
- **Visual:** Simple side-by-side diagram: normal tendon length vs elongated heel raise.

### Slide 3 — Answer (why)

- **Headline:** WHY IT HAPPENS
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Removing wedges too fast, walking without protection too soon, or stretching aggressively early on can let the healing tendon lengthen. The boot exists to hold length while it mends.
- **Visual:** Boot with wedges highlighted; arrow showing premature dorsiflexion blocked.

### Slide 4 — Answer (what helps)

- **Headline:** PROTECT LENGTH — FOLLOW THE PROTOCOL
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Follow your wedge-removal schedule exactly — never rush it. Keep protected plantarflexion (tip-toe position) until your team clears the next step.
- **Visual:** Calendar with wedge milestones marked, checkmarks.

### Slide 5 — Answer (stretching)

- **Headline:** DON'T FORCE STRETCHES EARLY
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Aggressive calf stretching before the tendon has fully healed can pull it longer. Tightness in the first year is normal — it usually resolves on its own.
- **Visual:** Gentle vs aggressive stretch comparison, cross on aggressive.

### Final slide — Close

- **Headline:** LENGTH MATTERS AS MUCH AS STRENGTH
- **Series label:** ATR RECOVERY SERIES
- **Body:** Avoiding re-rupture is only half the job. Protecting tendon length now pays off when you're back on your feet.
- **Visual:** Patient mid-walk outdoors, strong push-off implied.
