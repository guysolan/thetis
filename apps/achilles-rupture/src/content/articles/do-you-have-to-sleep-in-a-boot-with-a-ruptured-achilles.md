## Why your foot must stay toes-down 24/7

When you rupture your Achilles tendon, the two torn ends need to stay close together while new tissue forms between them. Your walking boot (or cast) holds your ankle in **plantarflexion** — toes pointing down — to keep those ends aligned.

That protection is not just for daytime. At night, you cannot control what your foot does. Without protection, your ankle can dorsiflex (bend upwards) during sleep — and if that happens, the repairing tendon can be **torn apart again**.

If you remove the boot and stand up, the ankle will bend upwards and the repairing tendon is torn again. The same risk applies in bed.

Most protocols require **24/7 protection** throughout the boot phase (typically weeks 1–12). Check with your own medical team, but the default answer to "do I have to sleep in my boot?" is **yes — or in an equivalent device that keeps the same angle**.

## Snakes and ladders: one mistake can undo weeks of healing

Your recovery is like the game Snakes and Ladders — each week sees you closer to the finish. But one small mistake is like landing on the big snake that takes you right back to the beginning, with a re-rupture.

Sleeping without protection is one of the most common causes of re-rupture or tendon elongation during the boot phase. You might feel fine during the day and think a few unprotected hours at night will not matter. It can.

Re-rupture rates are low overall (around 3–5%), but the consequences are serious: you go back to square one, often with surgery, and months of progress are lost.

## What happens to the tendon while you sleep

During the first weeks after rupture, your body lays down new collagen between the tendon ends. This tissue is weak — it cannot withstand the forces of normal walking, let alone an uncontrolled stretch at night.

Research on tendon healing shows three overlapping phases:

1. **Inflammation** (days 1–7): the body responds to injury
2. **Proliferation** (weeks 1–6): new tissue forms between the torn ends
3. **Remodelling** (months 3–18): the tissue gradually strengthens

During phases 1 and 2 — the entire boot phase — the repair is vulnerable. Unprotected dorsiflexion can cause **re-rupture** (the ends separate again) or **elongation** (the tendon heals too long, which permanently weakens your calf push-off).

## The problem with sleeping in a walking boot

Around **80% of patients** struggle to sleep in their boot. That is not surprising — walking boots were designed for walking, not sleeping.

Common complaints include:

- **Weight and bulk** — boots weigh 1–2 kg and feel like sleeping with a brick on your leg
- **Heat and sweating** — enclosed plastic and padding trap warmth
- **Dirty sheets** — the boot scuffs bedding and tracks in debris
- **Pressure points** — rigid edges dig into your calf and shin
- **Sleep deprivation** — poor sleep increases pain perception and makes everything harder to cope with

Poor sleep is not just uncomfortable. Exhaustion affects your mood, your ability to follow your rehab plan, and your overall recovery experience.

## When can you use a night splint instead?

A **night splint** is a lighter device that holds your foot in the same toes-down position as your boot, but is designed specifically for sleeping and washing.

**Timing matters.** Clinical guidance is:

- **Non-surgical treatment:** a night splint can usually be used from when your boot is fitted
- **After surgery:** typically from **2 weeks post-surgery**, once your wound has healed — your surgeon needs to confirm this

Always check with your clinician before switching. Some protocols require 24/7 boot wear for longer, especially in the very first weeks.

## Better to be too pointed than not enough

If you are ever unsure about your foot position, **err on the side of more plantarflexion** (more toes-down) rather than less.

A foot that is slightly too pointed is unlikely to cause harm. A foot that dorsiflexes even briefly can undo weeks of healing. This is why clinicians are strict about 24/7 protection — and why removing your boot "just for a few minutes" at night is never worth the risk.

## Practical tips if you are sleeping in your boot

If you are not yet cleared for a night splint, these strategies can help:

**Positioning**

- Sleep on your back with your leg elevated on pillows
- Support your calf, not just your foot
- Keep your leg above heart level to reduce swelling
- Avoid stomach sleeping — it can put pressure on your foot

**Temperature**

- Keep the room cool — boots trap heat
- Use light bedding rather than a heavy duvet
- Merino wool socks inside the boot regulate temperature better than cotton

**Safety**

- Keep crutches by your bed for night-time bathroom trips
- Use a nightlight — safer when you are half-asleep and hopping
- Empty your bladder before bed to reduce trips
- Adjust boot straps so they are snug but not painfully tight

Sleep disruption is normal in the first week. It improves as swelling settles and you find your best position. Most people find sleep returns to normal within a few weeks of boot removal.

## When to seek help

Contact your clinician if:

- You accidentally slept without protection and felt your ankle bend upwards
- You notice a sudden "pop" or sharp pain in your calf or heel
- Your foot position in the boot or splint does not feel secure
- You develop new calf swelling, warmth, or breathlessness (possible blood clot — see our article on [venous thromboembolism risk](/articles/venous-thromboembolism))

**If you think you may have re-ruptured your tendon, contact your medical team urgently.** Do not wait until your next scheduled appointment.
