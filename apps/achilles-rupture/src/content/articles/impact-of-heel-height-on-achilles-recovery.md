Managing heel height is critical for Achilles tendon recovery. Using heel lifts can reduce tendon strain, prevent elongation, and improve healing outcomes. Key points include:

- **Heel lifts reduce stress on the tendon**: A 12-mm orthotic lift decreases strain during walking by shifting the ankle into a plantar-flexed position. This is vital for non-surgical recovery, where keeping the tendon ends close is crucial.
- **Improper heel height risks permanent damage**: Tendon elongation from incorrect heel management can lower ankle strength and mobility long-term. Patients with over 30% heel-rise deficits a year post-injury show reduced ankle power even six years later.
- **Gradual adjustments are necessary**: Heel lifts must be reduced layer by layer to avoid calf muscle shortening or tendon elongation. Early recovery typically involves a [plaster cast or CAM boot](https://achilles-rupture.com/articles/plaster-vs-boot/) with wedges, transitioning to smaller lifts over time.
- **Footwear matters**: During recovery, avoid flat shoes. Use running shoes with a 10-mm offset or add a 12-mm orthotic lift to reduce tendon tension.

Proper heel height management ensures the tendon heals correctly, minimizing complications and supporting long-term functionality.

## Research Findings on Heel Height and Recovery

### Heel Height and Reduced Tendon Strain

Adjusting heel height plays a crucial role in protecting tendons during recovery and restoring function. Research shows that elevating the heel reduces tendon stress while walking. For example, using a 12-mm orthotic heel lift significantly lessens tendon strain, whereas standard running shoes with a 10-mm offset may actually increase tendon loading compared to walking barefoot. This underscores the importance of proper heel elevation when transitioning back to regular footwear after an injury.

A 12-week randomized trial conducted at [La Trobe University](https://www.latrobe.edu.au/) in Melbourne compared the effectiveness of 12-mm heel lifts with a traditional eccentric exercise program in patients suffering from mid-portion Achilles tendinopathy. The results? Participants using heel lifts improved their VISA-A scores by 26.0 points, compared to a 17.4-point improvement in the eccentric exercise group. Researcher Chantel L. Rabusin highlighted the findings:

> In adults with mid-portion Achilles tendinopathy, heel lifts were more effective than calf muscle eccentric exercise in reducing pain and improving function at 12 weeks.

These findings provide a biomechanical basis for improving heel-rise performance during recovery.

### Effects on Heel-Rise Height and Strength Recovery

Tendon elongation is a key factor in functional recovery. After an Achilles rupture, patients often show a side-to-side tendon length difference of 2.6 to 3.1 cm compared to their uninjured leg. This difference can lead to significant deficits in maximal heel-rise height. A long-term study revealed that a heel-rise deficit exceeding 30% one year post-injury is associated with much lower ankle power than deficits under 15%.

Heel-rise performance metrics - such as height, number of repetitions, and total work - are strong indicators of recovery. Total work, measured in joules, has been shown to correlate with critical gait parameters, including walking speed (r = 0.57), peak ankle power (r = 0.66), and stance phase duration (r = -0.63).

### Heel Height in Surgical vs. Non-Surgical Treatment

Both surgical and non-surgical treatments emphasize the importance of controlling heel height during recovery. For the first 10 weeks, the foot is typically kept in a plantar-flexed, "tip-toe" position to ensure the tendon ends remain close together. This approach is highly effective for acute ruptures treated promptly, with about 90% of patients achieving excellent outcomes through conservative management. [Surgery is generally reserved for the remaining 10% of cases](https://achilles-rupture.com/articles/why-surgery-isnt-usually-needed-for-achilles-tendon-ruptures/) or for chronic ruptures left untreated for more than four weeks.

Preventing tendon elongation is critical, regardless of the treatment method. Studies have shown that the Achilles Tendon Resting Angle (ATRA) is a reliable indicator of tendon stretching and correlates with reduced heel-rise work (r = -0.44 to -0.59) in both surgical and non-surgical cases. Even with surgical repair, failing to manage heel height properly can lead to permanent tendon lengthening and functional impairments. For chronic ruptures, surgical procedures often involve deliberate tendon shortening to restore proper length and improve calf muscle function.

### Recommended Heel Elevation for Early Recovery

To support tendon healing and reduce strain during early recovery, specific heel adjustments are often recommended. The initial phase typically involves positioning the ankle at about 30° of plantar flexion within a CAM walker boot. Heel wedges inside the boot allow for a gradual decrease in plantar flexion over time. By week 5, patients usually start removing these wedges incrementally, following their healthcare provider's guidance. Once the boot is removed - typically around weeks 6–8 - the [University of Delaware](https://www.udel.edu/) advises using a 1/4-inch (6.35 mm) heel lift in both shoes for up to 11 weeks post-injury. This approach ensures balance between the injured and uninjured sides, promoting a more natural gait and minimizing the risk of secondary complications.

For individuals dealing with Achilles tendinopathy rather than a rupture, studies indicate that a 12 mm in-shoe orthotic heel lift can significantly decrease the tendon’s tensile load during walking. In cases of insertional Achilles tendinopathy, a 2 cm (20 mm) heel lift has been shown to reduce pain and improve symptoms within just two weeks.

These carefully calibrated heel adjustments provide a foundation for understanding how orthopedic devices further aid recovery.

### Orthopedic Boots and Heel Inserts

Orthopedic boots, such as [Aircast](https://dub.sh/c10uDlA) or [VACOped](https://dub.sh/NOaCy2D), are designed with adjustable heel wedges, allowing for gradual reduction of plantar flexion. These boots enable early weightbearing while maintaining the necessary plantarflexion position. For detailed comparisons of these boots, the [Achilles Rupture Recovery](https://achilles-rupture.com/) website offers helpful resources to guide patients in choosing the best option for their recovery.

Another option is the Achilles Rupture Splint by [Thetis Medical](https://thetismedical.com/), which ensures proper heel elevation throughout recovery. [Thetis Medical](https://thetismedical.com/) provides extensive resources, including guides on the [Achilles rupture timeline](https://www.thetismedical.com/FAQs/achilles-rupture-timeline) and [torn Achilles recovery](https://www.thetismedical.com/FAQs/torn-achilles-recovery). Their advice underscores the importance of maintaining correct heel positioning, as they caution:

> One mistake can take you right back to square one... If the position is lost then the tendon will stretch or tear again.

When transitioning back to running, selecting footwear with a heel-to-toe drop of at least 9 mm is recommended. Standard running shoes, which often have a 10 mm heel offset, can increase Achilles tendon load compared to walking barefoot. To counter this, adding a 12 mm orthotic lift may help reduce tension.

### Balancing Strain Reduction and Muscle Shortening

While tools like adjustable boots and inserts are effective in lowering tendon strain, they come with the risk of calf muscle shortening if used for too long. Elevated heel lifts reduce tensile load on the tendon but can lead to shortening of the muscle–tendon unit due to prolonged plantar flexion. This creates a careful balancing act: if heel elevation is maintained for too long, it may cause calf muscle shortening, but removing it too quickly could risk tendon elongation.

The solution lies in gradually tapering heel height according to structured, evidence-based protocols. Healthcare providers play a key role in monitoring progress, ensuring heel elevation is reduced systematically while tracking performance in activities like heel rises.

## Long-Term Effects on Tendon Health

### Preventing Tendon Elongation and Re-Rupture

Tendon elongation can seriously hinder recovery by permanently reducing both strength and mobility. Research highlights that side-to-side tendon length differences of 2.6–3.1 cm and heel-rise deficits exceeding 30% (linked to 114% vs. 106% disparities) are strong indicators of long-term functional issues. Karin Grävare Silbernagel emphasizes the importance of addressing this:

> Minimizing tendon elongation and regaining heel-rise height may be important for the long-term recovery of ankle biomechanics, particularly during more demanding activities such as jumping.

Elevated heel heights during recovery play a key role in reducing tendon tensile load. Additionally, improved calf muscle endurance and total heel-rise work show a moderate connection to better ankle biomechanics and power during walking. These factors underline the importance of careful footwear choices as patients progress through recovery to avoid unnecessary stress on the tendon.

### Transitioning to Regular Footwear

The shift from controlled heel elevation to regular footwear is a crucial step in protecting the tendon as it continues to heal. Around week 10, patients typically begin transitioning out of [Aircast vs VACOped orthopedic boots](https://achilles-rupture.com/articles/aircast-vs-vacoped/). However, completely flat shoes should be avoided, as the tendon remains vulnerable, and a sudden drop in heel height could lead to permanent elongation. Research suggests that a gradual reduction in heel elevation is the safest way to preserve tendon integrity.

Most running shoes come with a built-in heel offset of about 10 mm. Adding an in-shoe orthotic lift of 12 mm can further reduce tension on the tendon. Patients are also advised to avoid calf stretches for several months after leaving the boot - even if the tendon feels tight - to allow the tissue to mature fully. The [Achilles rupture timeline](https://www.thetismedical.com/FAQs/achilles-rupture-timeline) from Thetis Medical provides detailed guidance on managing this sensitive phase. Additionally, protective footwear should be used in situations where sudden dorsiflexion could occur, helping to prevent accidental elongation. For more information on footwear options and strategies, the [Achilles Rupture Recovery](https://achilles-rupture.com) website is a valuable resource for navigating this stage of recovery safely.

## Conclusion

### The Importance of Evidence-Based Heel Height Adjustments

Managing heel height properly plays a key role in achieving full recovery after an Achilles injury. Research shows that using a 12 mm in-shoe orthotic heel lift can significantly reduce the load on the tendon during the early stages of rehabilitation. On the other hand, neglecting proper heel height management can lead to permanent tendon elongation, reduced heel-rise height, and diminished ankle power. Studies highlight that patients with heel-rise height differences greater than 30% after one year often experience lower ankle kinetics (LSI 70%–149%) even six years later. Karin Grävare Silbernagel, PT, PhD, stresses:

> Minimizing tendon elongation appears to be an important treatment goal when aiming for full return of function.

This evidence highlights the importance of practical, [evidence-based recommendations](https://achilles-rupture.com/articles/evidence-based-achilles-rupture-recovery/) for patients and healthcare providers.

### Recommendations for Patients and Healthcare Providers

For patients, the first 10 weeks are critical, and following [essential recovery tips](https://achilles-rupture.com/patients/nine-tips-for-achilles-tendon-rupture/) can help manage this period. During this time, keeping the foot in a constant plantarflexed position is essential, as even a single upward movement can disrupt the healing tendon. When transitioning out of the boot around week 10, avoid flat shoes entirely. Instead, opt for standard running shoes with a 12 mm orthotic lift. Additionally, calf stretches should be avoided for several months post-boot to prevent permanent elongation of the tendon.

Healthcare providers should prioritize the single-leg standing heel-rise test at the one-year mark to assess recovery progress. A difference of more than 30% in heel-rise height signals a higher risk of biomechanical issues. Monitoring for calf muscle atrophy is equally important, as it strongly predicts reduced ankle plantarflexion strength at six months.

## FAQs

### How do I know my heel lift is too high or too low?

Your heel lift might be too high if it creates discomfort, makes you feel unsteady, or changes the way you walk. On the other hand, if it’s too low, it might not effectively ease tension on your Achilles tendon or relieve symptoms. Studies indicate that a lift of about **12 mm** can help reduce stress on the tendon during walking. The key is to choose a lift that feels comfortable, promotes natural movement, and aligns with your recovery plan. For the best results, consult a healthcare professional to ensure it’s suited to your needs.

### When is it safe to start lowering heel wedges or stopping lifts?

The timing for removing heel lifts varies based on your recovery progress. These lifts help reduce tension on the Achilles tendon during the early stages of healing and are often used for **3 to 6 months**. You can safely transition away from them once the tendon demonstrates enough strength, your walking pattern (gait) is normal, and there’s no pain or swelling. Be sure to consult a healthcare professional to evaluate your readiness and guide you through the process.

### What shoe heel-to-toe drop is best after I’m out of the boot?

When moving out of a walking boot, wearing shoes with a heel-to-toe drop of about 12 mm can help ease the strain on your Achilles tendon. This setup reduces stress on the tendon while you walk, which can support your recovery. Initially, a higher drop is better, and you can gradually transition to a lower drop as your strength builds. Avoid switching to low or zero-drop shoes too quickly, as they can put extra pressure on the tendon. Always check with your healthcare provider for advice specific to your situation.
