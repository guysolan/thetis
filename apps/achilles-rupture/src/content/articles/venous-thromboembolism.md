**Key message:** blood clots (venous thromboembolism, or VTE) are a serious but under-discussed risk after Achilles rupture. Know the warning signs, discuss blood thinners with your medical team, and stay as active as your protocol allows.

## What is VTE?

**Venous thromboembolism (VTE)** is the umbrella term for blood clots that form in the deep veins of the leg. There are two stages you need to know about:

**Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)** — a clot in the deep veins of your calf or thigh. It can cause pain, swelling, and long-term vein damage. Many small clots cause no symptoms at all.

**Pulmonary embolism (PE)** — what happens if part of that clot breaks free and travels to your lungs. A PE blocks blood flow in the lungs and can be **life-threatening**. It requires emergency treatment.

| | DVT (leg clot) | PE (lung clot) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Where** | Deep veins of the calf or thigh | Arteries of the lungs |
| **Severity** | Painful but treatable | Medical emergency |
| **Key signs** | Calf pain, one-leg swelling, warmth | Breathlessness, chest pain, fainting |
| **Action** | Contact your clinician urgently | Call emergency services immediately |

## Why Achilles rupture is different

When you rupture your Achilles tendon, the focus is naturally on the tendon itself. But there is a second risk that gets far less attention: **blood clots**.

Your calf muscles act as a pump. Every time you walk, the muscles contract and squeeze blood upwards through your veins. When your leg is immobilised in a walking boot, this pump is largely switched off.

At the same time, the injury causes inflammation near the deep veins of the calf, which can make your blood more prone to clotting. The combination — **reduced blood flow plus increased clotting tendency** — creates ideal conditions for a clot to form.

Of all lower leg injuries, Achilles tendon rupture is probably the one with the highest rate of thrombosis problems. Studies vary widely in how they look for clots (some use ultrasound screening, others only count symptomatic cases), so reported rates range from under 1% to over 60%. What is clear is that the risk is real and worth taking seriously.

## What the UK-FATE study found

A large UK study — the **UK Foot and Ankle Thrombo-Embolism (UK-FATE) audit** — investigated VTE rates after foot and ankle surgery and injury across 68 participating centres.

> **Solan M, Briggs-Price S, Houchen-Wolloff L, et al.** Incidence of Venous Thromboembolism Following Achilles Tendon Rupture. Data from the UK Foot and Ankle Thrombo-Embolism (UK-FATE) Audit. _Injury._ 2025;56(3):112212. [doi:10.1016/j.injury.2025.112212](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.injury.2025.112212)

### Key findings

- **Significantly higher risk:** people who experience an Achilles rupture are about **6.5 times more likely** to develop a symptomatic VTE compared to people recovering from other foot and ankle surgeries.
- **VTE rate:** 3.69% of ATR patients developed a symptomatic VTE within 90 days. This compares to only 0.57% for other foot and ankle procedures.
- **Age matters:** older patients were more likely to develop VTE. The average age of those who developed a clot was 54, compared to 48 for those who did not.
- **Surgery vs non-surgery:** whether the rupture was treated surgically or non-surgically did not significantly change VTE risk (2.53% surgical vs 4.09% non-surgical — not statistically significant).
- **Immobilisation approach:** different strategies (cast vs boot, early vs delayed weight-bearing) did not show a significant difference in symptomatic VTE rates in this study.
- **Blood thinners:** 86.7% of patients received chemical thromboprophylaxis (usually for about 6 weeks), but the study did not find a statistically significant difference in VTE rates between those who received blood thinners and those who did not. This does not prove blood thinners are ineffective — it means more research is needed, and individual risk assessment still matters.

The fact that foot and ankle surgeons created a special category for Achilles rupture patients in this national audit shows the profession recognises this injury carries a raised clot risk.

### Why the higher risk?

The exact reasons are not fully understood, but several factors likely contribute:

- **Inflammation** from the injury itself, close to the deep calf veins
- **Immobility** — any period of reduced movement increases clot risk
- **Impaired calf pump** — you cannot push off normally while in a boot
- **Age** — ATRs are becoming more common in an ageing, active population

## Warning signs you must know

Your injured leg will already be swollen and uncomfortable. What you are looking for is a **change** — new symptoms that feel different from your normal post-rupture discomfort.

### DVT warning signs (leg)

- **New calf pain** — often a deep, cramping ache that feels different from your Achilles injury
- **Swelling in one leg only**, particularly above the rupture site towards the knee
- **Warmth or redness** in the calf area
- **Heavy or tight feeling** in the affected leg

Swelling of the whole leg up to the knee should prompt medical review even if painless.

### PE warning signs (lungs) — call emergency services

- **Sudden shortness of breath** that you cannot explain
- **Sharp chest pain**, especially when breathing in
- **Feeling faint or lightheaded**
- **Coughing up blood** (rare but serious)
- **Rapid heartbeat**

**If you experience any combination of these lung symptoms, call emergency services immediately.** Do not wait to see if it passes.

## Blood thinners during recovery

Most hospitals will assess your clot risk after an Achilles rupture and prescribe **blood thinners** (anticoagulants) if appropriate. No-one really knows the best duration to take blood thinners for, but **4–6 weeks** covering the full equinus (toes-down) boot phase is a common approach.

### Injections vs tablets

Daily injections (e.g. enoxaparin) are well-established and commonly used for a week or two. For Achilles rupture, where the clot risk lasts longer, **oral tablets** (e.g. rivaroxaban or apixaban) are often preferred for compliance — they are easier to stick with than 6 weeks of self-injections.

One UK hospital that switched from injections to tablets saw thrombosis rates fall from 6% to 0% in Achilles rupture patients. Tablets are not officially licensed for this specific indication everywhere, but many hospitals use them with pharmacy approval because the practical benefits are obvious.

Discuss with your medical team:

- Whether you need blood thinners at all (based on your personal risk factors)
- Whether injections or tablets are right for you
- How long to take them for
- What to do if you miss a dose

Patients who suffer a thrombosis during Achilles rupture recovery may also fare worse in terms of long-term leg function — another reason to take prevention seriously.

## What you can do to reduce your risk

Blood thinners are one part of the picture. You can also help keep blood moving:

1. **Walk in your boot as allowed** — early weight-bearing activates the calf muscle pump. Walk as much as your protocol allows.
2. **Take blood thinners exactly as prescribed** — do not stop early without discussing it with your clinician.
3. **Stay alert to symptoms** — blood thinners reduce risk but do not eliminate it entirely.
4. **Keep your leg elevated** when resting, especially in the first two weeks after surgery.
5. **Ask about a Doppler ultrasound** — some teams check your veins at the time of your tendon scan.
6. **Wear your boot or night splint as instructed** — but do not use immobilisation as an excuse to stay sedentary.

## Study limitations

It is important to understand what the UK-FATE study can and cannot tell us:

- **Symptomatic VTE only** — some people may have had clots without symptoms, so the true rate may be higher.
- **Observational design** — this study cannot prove cause and effect. It cannot definitively say whether blood thinners prevent VTE after ATR.
- **Missing data** — some variables (e.g. BMI) were not collected.
- **Chronic ruptures excluded** — the study did not include late-presenting or chronic ATR.

Further research is needed to determine the optimal strategies for preventing and managing VTE after Achilles rupture. In the meantime, awareness of the risk and prompt medical attention for any symptoms are crucial.
