**Achilles rupture return to sport criteria** should be based on what your leg can do, not just how many months have passed. Six months after injury, many patients feel much better, but the tendon, calf, balance system, and confidence are often still rebuilding.

That is normal. Return to sport is usually a **criteria-based decision**. The calendar helps guide expectations, but it should not be the only reason you run, jump, sprint, or change direction again.

## Key takeaways

- Return to sport after Achilles rupture should be **criteria-based, not purely time-based**.
- Six months is often a rebuilding phase, not automatic clearance for full sport.
- A common late-stage benchmark is **25+ high-quality single-leg heel raises**.
- You also need good balance, pain-free walking/running/jumping, and strong side-to-side symmetry.
- Psychological readiness matters because fear and hesitation can affect movement quality.
- Sudden pain, a new pop, or loss of push-off should prompt urgent medical review.

## Why time alone is not enough

The biggest mistake is assuming that "6 months" or "9 months" means the tendon is ready for sport. Two people can be at the same date and have very different strength, balance, confidence, and tendon capacity.

Research backs up this caution. A scoping review of 34 studies on return to sport after Achilles tendon repair found that studies often reported time to return, ankle movement, calf size, and heel-rise testing, but **none clearly defined a measurement as a formal return-to-sport clearance criterion**. In other words, the research world still has gaps, and clinicians often rely on a structured set of practical tests.

This is why your physiotherapist may test your heel raises, balance, hop control, strength symmetry, and confidence rather than simply saying, "You are 6 months out, go play."

## The main return-to-sport criteria

Different clinicians and sports use different thresholds, but the common categories are consistent: strength, control, pain-free loading, symmetry, and confidence.

| Criteria | What it usually means | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **25+ single-leg heel raises** | Repeated strong reps with good height and control | Tests calf endurance and Achilles push-off capacity |
| **Good balance and control** | Stable single-leg stance and controlled movement | Reduces sloppy loading and trip risk |
| **Pain-free loading** | Walking, running drills, and jumping without flare | Shows the tendon is tolerating higher demand |
| **Strength symmetry** | At least about 85% compared with the uninjured side | Reduces reliance on compensation |
| **Psychological confidence** | Trusting the leg during sport-like movement | Helps avoid hesitant, protective mechanics |

These criteria are not a home clearance test. They are a discussion framework for you and your physiotherapist or surgeon.

## What 25 single-leg heel raises really means

The single-leg heel raise is one of the most useful late-stage Achilles tests because it asks one calf and tendon to lift your full bodyweight repeatedly.

But the number alone can mislead. A poor-quality set of 25 shallow reps is not the same as 25 strong reps with good heel height.

Look for:

- Full, controlled heel rise rather than tiny pulses.
- Similar heel height to the uninjured side.
- Smooth lowering without bouncing.
- No knee bend or trunk lean to cheat the movement.
- No next-day swelling, limp, or sharp tendon pain.

Heel-rise research shows why quality matters. In one study, patients looked much closer to normal when only repetitions were counted, but still had clear deficits when total heel-rise work was measured. That means height and power matter, not just the rep count.

## Pain-free running and jumping

Before sport, daily walking should already be comfortable and efficient. If you still limp when tired, struggle on stairs, or get next-day swelling after a longer walk, full sport is usually too much.

Return-to-sport loading often progresses in layers:

1. Pain-free walking with normal gait.
2. Controlled calf strengthening and balance.
3. Running drills or return-to-run work.
4. Low-level jumping and landing.
5. Direction change, acceleration, and sport-specific drills.
6. Full training, then competition or match play.

You should not jump from walking better straight into a match, race, or court session. The Achilles has to tolerate repeated stretch-shortening loads, which are much higher than normal walking.

## Strength symmetry and confidence

Strength symmetry means comparing the injured side with the uninjured side. Thetis guidance uses **at least 85% symmetry** as a sport clearance benchmark. Some high-demand protocols may aim closer to **90%**, especially for jumping, sprinting, or cutting sports.

Symmetry may include:

- Calf strength testing.
- Heel-rise height and endurance.
- Hop or jump tests where appropriate.
- Balance or control tests.
- Running mechanics.

Confidence matters too. A 2023 study found that a psychological readiness score could help assess return-to-sport readiness after Achilles tendon repair. That fits what patients describe in real life: even when strength is improving, fear of re-injury can change how you move.

If you feel frightened loading the tendon, that does not mean you are weak-minded. It means confidence needs to be rebuilt with graded exposure, clear criteria, and support from your rehab team.

## What this means for your recovery

At 6 months, many patients are improving well but still rebuilding tendon capacity. That is not failure. It is the normal late stage of a major tendon injury.

Use this checklist with your clinician:

| Before full sport, ask | Good sign |
| --- | --- |
| Can I walk and use stairs without a limp? | Normal gait and confident stair control |
| Can I do repeated single-leg heel raises? | 25+ quality reps, or a clear plan toward them |
| Is my strength close enough side-to-side? | At least 85% symmetry for sport clearance |
| Can I run and jump without flare? | No sharp pain, swelling, or next-day limp |
| Do I trust the leg under load? | Confidence during sport-like drills |

Seek urgent review if you feel a new pop, sudden sharp Achilles pain, a new gap in the tendon, or a sudden loss of push-off. Whole-leg swelling, calf pain, breathlessness, or chest pain should also be reviewed urgently because of blood clot risk.

## Frequently asked questions

### When can I return to sport after Achilles rupture?

Return to sport is often around 9 months or later, but the date is not enough. You should meet functional criteria such as strong heel raises, good balance, pain-free loading, strength symmetry, and clinician clearance.

### Is 6 months too early to return to sport after Achilles rupture?

For many patients, 6 months is still a rebuilding phase. Some may start higher-level preparation, but most should not return to full sport unless they meet objective criteria and their clinician agrees.

### How many single-leg heel raises should I do before sport?

A common late-stage benchmark is 25 or more high-quality single-leg heel raises, with good heel height and control compared with the uninjured side.

### What strength symmetry do I need after Achilles rupture?

Many pathways look for strong side-to-side symmetry before sport. Thetis guidance uses at least 85% symmetry for sport clearance, while some high-demand protocols may aim closer to 90%.

### Why does confidence matter before returning to sport?

Confidence matters because hesitation, fear, and protective movement can change how you load the tendon. Psychological readiness should be considered alongside strength, balance, and pain-free movement.
