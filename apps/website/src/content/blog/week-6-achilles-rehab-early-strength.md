If you are looking up **week 6 Achilles rehab**, this is usually the point where recovery starts to feel more active. Many protocols introduce **early strengthening** around this stage, but the key idea is often misunderstood. This is **controlled, protected loading** of a still-vulnerable tendon, not a green light for normal calf training, deep stretches, or unrestricted exercise.

At this point the tendon is moving out of the most fragile early period, but it can still **elongate** if you load it in the wrong position or progress too fast. That is why **foot position**, **heel wedges**, and **gradual progression** matter more than the exercise name itself.

## Key Takeaways

- **Week 6 Achilles rehab** often introduces the first true strengthening work, but it should be treated as **protected loading**, not unrestricted exercise.
- **Seated calf raises** are commonly used because they let you load the tendon in a controlled way while keeping the foot in **plantarflexion** with a wedge or block.
- The goal is to load the tendon **without** letting the ankle drift into **unprotected dorsiflexion**, which can increase elongation risk.
- **Position matters as much as load**. A light load in a bad position can be less safe than a moderate load in a protected one.
- Progression should usually be **slow**: bodyweight or very light resistance first, then more load only if swelling, pain, and tendon response stay calm.
- If you are unsure about timing, follow your own clinician's plan. Protocols vary, and this stage is one of the easiest to overdo.

## Why Week 6 Is a Turning Point

Around week 6, many patients move from "protect at all costs" to "start using it a bit more." That shift is real, but it is still easy to misunderstand.

The tendon has already formed a healing bridge, but it is not mature yet. In the [early healing phase](/blog/achilles-tendon-healing-early-protection), new collagen is still organising, and the repair remains easier to stretch than a normal tendon. That means loading is useful, but only if the **position and dose** are appropriate.

This is why week 6 is not the time to start:

- Standing calf raises on a flat foot
- Stretching the heel down off a step
- Copying a gym programme from a healthy athlete
- Skipping ahead because the ankle feels "pretty good"

Think of this phase as **teaching the tendon to tolerate load** while still protecting its length.

## What Early Strength Work Actually Means

When clinicians talk about early strengthening after an Achilles rupture, they usually do **not** mean full, barefoot calf raises. They mean carefully controlled exercises that add load while keeping the tendon in a safer position.

Common early options include:

- **Seated calf raises** with the foot still supported in plantarflexion
- Gentle **isometric plantarflexion** work
- Light resistance exercises that avoid pulling the ankle upward

The point is not to show how strong you are. The point is to introduce a small, repeatable stimulus the tendon can handle.

| Exercise approach | What it is trying to do | Why it can be useful |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Seated calf raise with wedge** | Load the tendon while the foot stays toes-down | Protects approximation while beginning strength work |
| **Isometric plantarflexion** | Create calf tension without large movement | Lets you start loading with good control |
| **Gradual external load** | Increase demand bit by bit | Builds tolerance without sudden overload |

This is why the phrase **"early strength work"** can sound more advanced than it really is. At week 6, early strengthening is still a form of **rehab precision**, not a fitness challenge.

## Why Seated Calf Raises Use a Wedge

The wedge is not there to make the exercise easier. It is there to make the exercise **safer for that stage**.

Keeping the foot in **plantarflexion** (toes down) helps preserve **tendon approximation**. In plain English, it keeps the healing tendon ends in a shorter, more protected relationship than a flat or toes-up position. That matters because week 6 is still part of the window where **tendon elongation** can quietly occur if the tendon is loaded while being stretched.

## Safe Loading vs Risky Loading

This is the simplest way to think about the week 6 phase:

| Safer early loading | Riskier early loading |
| --- | --- |
| **Seated** work with the foot kept **toes down** | Flat-foot or toes-up loading |
| **Small** increases in load | Big jumps in resistance |
| Stopping if pain or swelling clearly rises | "Pushing through" because it is meant to be hard |
| Loading prescribed by your clinician or physio | Improvising from generic online rehab videos |
| Respecting wedge and angle rules | Removing supports because you feel ready |

The important bit is not whether the exercise is technically in or out of the boot. The important bit is whether the tendon is still being loaded in a **protected position**.

Some teams start these exercises around week 6. Others delay based on:

- Pain and swelling
- Whether you had surgery
- Tendon gap or approximation concerns
- Previous healing problems
- Overall confidence and compliance

That variation is normal. There is no single universal "week 6" exercise list that overrides your own medical team.

## What This Means for Your Recovery

If your programme introduces strengthening around week 6, treat it like a **dose-controlled medicine**, not a performance test.

1. **Use the position you were given.** If your foot is meant to be on a wedge, keep it there.
2. **Start lighter than your ego wants.** This phase is about tendon response, not gym numbers.
3. **Watch the next 24 hours.** Pain that settles is one thing; swelling, sharp pain, or loss of confidence in the tendon is another.
4. **Do not combine strength work with early stretching.** Protected loading and unprotected dorsiflexion are very different things.
5. **Ask what progression looks like before you improvise.** Good rehab is boringly specific.

If nights in the boot or splint are still part of your protocol, keep taking that seriously. Night-time slips into dorsiflexion can undo the same length protection you are trying to preserve during rehab. If the boot is intolerable, ask about an [Achilles night splint](/achilles-rupture-splint).

### When to get urgent advice

- A new **pop** or **snap**
- Sharp pain during loading that feels wrong, not just hard work
- Sudden loss of push-off strength
- A major increase in swelling or symptoms like the original rupture

If you are not sure whether a reaction is normal, contact your physio, fracture clinic, or surgeon before progressing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I start calf raises at week 6 after an Achilles rupture?

Some protocols introduce early calf strengthening around week 6, but only in a protected way and only if your clinician says the tendon is ready. This is not the same as normal gym calf raises or stretching off a step.

### Why do early seated calf raises use a wedge?

The wedge keeps the foot in plantarflexion (**toes down**), which helps protect tendon approximation and reduces how much the healing tendon is stretched during loading.

### Is early strengthening done in or out of the boot?

It depends on the protocol. Some teams begin with protected loading out of the boot in a seated position using wedges or blocks; others keep strengthening inside the boot first or delay it based on pain, swelling, tendon response, and overall risk.

### What should I avoid during week 6 Achilles rehab?

Avoid unprotected dorsiflexion, flat-foot calf raises, stretching the heel below neutral, and adding too much weight too quickly. This stage is about controlled loading, not testing the tendon.

### What symptoms mean I should stop and get advice?

A new pop or snap, sharp pain during loading, sudden loss of strength, marked swelling, or symptoms that feel like the original injury should prompt urgent medical review.
