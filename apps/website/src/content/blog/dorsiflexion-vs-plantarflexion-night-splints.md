If you search for "night splint" online, you will find dozens of options. Nearly all of them pull your toes **up** — a position called **dorsiflexion**. These splints are designed for **plantar fasciitis**.

If you have an **Achilles rupture**, you need the **opposite** position: toes pointed **down** (**plantarflexion**). Using a dorsiflexion splint after an Achilles rupture can pull the healing tendon apart and set your recovery back significantly.

This article explains the biomechanics behind each condition, why direction matters, and how to choose the right splint for your situation.

## Key Takeaways

- **Plantar fasciitis** requires **dorsiflexion** (toes up) — stretching the tight calf and plantar fascia overnight helps reduce morning pain
- **Achilles rupture** requires **plantarflexion** (toes down) — keeping the torn tendon ends close together allows healing tissue to bridge the gap
- **Most night splints** on the market are dorsiflexion devices designed for plantar fasciitis — they should **never** be used for Achilles rupture
- **The Thetis Night Splint** is unique: it holds plantarflexion (30°) and is patented specifically for Achilles rupture recovery
- **Using the wrong splint** can cause tendon elongation, delayed healing, or re-rupture

## The Two Opposite Conditions

The calf muscles, Achilles tendon, and plantar fascia form one connected chain. Think of the heel bone sitting in a hammock — the Achilles tendon is the rope on one end, and the plantar fascia is the rope on the other.

When something goes wrong at different points in this chain, the treatment direction is opposite:

| Condition | What's happening | Treatment direction | Why |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Plantar fasciitis** | Tight, disorganised fibres need stretching | Toes **up** (dorsiflexion) | Stretches the fascia, "combs out" the knots |
| **Achilles rupture** | Torn tendon ends need to heal together | Toes **down** (plantarflexion) | Brings tendon ends closer, protects healing tissue |

This is not a subtle difference — these are opposite biomechanical goals.

## Dorsiflexion Splints: For Plantar Fasciitis

Plantar fasciitis causes that classic "first-step pain" in the morning. After a night of rest, the plantar fascia tightens and contracts. When you stand up, those tight fibres are suddenly loaded — and it hurts.

### How dorsiflexion splints work

By holding the foot in a toes-up position overnight, dorsiflexion splints:

- Keep the plantar fascia and calf muscles in a gentle stretch
- Prevent the tissue from contracting into a shortened position
- Reduce the shock of that first morning step

### Common dorsiflexion options

There are many products that achieve this position:

- **Night socks** (Strasburg sock) — a fabric sock with a strap that pulls the toes up
- **Dorsiwedge splints** — rigid plastic shells that hold 90° or more
- **Boot-style splints** — enclosed designs with adjustable straps

These devices are widely available and often recommended for plantar fasciitis. The evidence is mixed — some patients find them helpful, others find them uncomfortable — but the mechanism is sound: **stretch** the tight tissue overnight.

## Plantarflexion Splints: For Achilles Rupture

Achilles rupture is a completely different injury. The tendon has torn — either partially or completely — and the body needs to build new tissue to bridge the gap between the torn ends.

### Why plantarflexion is essential

When your foot points down (plantarflexion):

- The calf muscle shortens
- The heel bone moves up
- The torn tendon ends come **closer together**

When your foot moves up (dorsiflexion):

- The calf muscle lengthens
- The heel bone moves down
- The torn tendon ends are **pulled apart**

During the early weeks of healing, the tissue bridging the gap is fragile. Repeated dorsiflexion — or worse, sleeping with your foot in dorsiflexion — can stretch this new tissue before it is strong. The result is **tendon elongation** (healing "long"), which reduces push-off strength long-term.

## Why Using the Wrong Splint Is Dangerous

If you have an Achilles rupture and use a dorsiflexion splint designed for plantar fasciitis:

1. **Your foot is pulled into the wrong position** — toes up instead of down
2. **The torn tendon ends are separated** — the opposite of what you need for healing
3. **New tissue is stretched before it's ready** — leading to elongation or failure
4. **Re-rupture risk increases** — especially in the early weeks

This is not theoretical. Unprotected dorsiflexion is one of the mechanisms that can cause re-rupture or healing in a lengthened position. A splint that actively pulls you into dorsiflexion makes this worse.

If you have plantar fasciitis and use a plantarflexion splint:

- It won't help — you need stretch, not protection
- It won't harm you — but it's a waste of money

The danger is one-directional: dorsiflexion splints can harm Achilles rupture patients. Plantarflexion splints are simply ineffective for plantar fasciitis.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I use a plantar fasciitis night splint for my Achilles rupture?

**No.** Plantar fasciitis splints hold dorsiflexion — the opposite of what a healing Achilles needs. Using one could pull the torn tendon ends apart, causing elongation or re-rupture. You need a plantarflexion splint like the [Thetis Night Splint](/achilles-rupture-splint).

### Why do plantar fasciitis splints pull the toes up?

The plantar fascia tightens overnight. Holding the foot in dorsiflexion keeps the tissue stretched, preventing it from contracting into a shortened position. This reduces the "first-step pain" that plantar fasciitis patients experience in the morning.

### Why do Achilles rupture splints point the toes down?

After an Achilles rupture, the torn ends need to stay close so healing tissue can bridge the gap. Plantarflexion (toes down) brings the ends closer. Dorsiflexion (toes up) pulls them apart — which can cause elongation or re-rupture during the fragile healing phase.

### What makes the Thetis splint different from other night splints?

The Thetis Night Splint is the only night splint designed specifically for Achilles rupture. It holds **30° plantarflexion** — the same angle as a walking boot. Most other night splints hold dorsiflexion for plantar fasciitis — the opposite direction.

### When can I start using a night splint after Achilles rupture?

Most protocols allow a plantarflexion night splint after the very early healing stage — usually **week 2–3** for non-surgical patients, or once the wound has healed for surgical patients. Always check with your clinician before switching from your boot.
